Why CPAP Users in Apartments Need a Plan

For the 30 million Americans who use CPAP therapy, a power outage isn't an inconvenience — it's a medical emergency. Without treatment, sleep apnea symptoms return immediately: interrupted breathing, oxygen desaturation, and increased cardiovascular strain.

Apartment dwellers face an extra challenge: gas generators are banned in most multi-unit buildings, and running one on a balcony can still let carbon monoxide seep into your unit — or your neighbor's. The CDC reports that CO poisoning from generators kills over 70 people annually, many in apartments.

A portable power station is the only safe, practical solution for apartment CPAP users. Here's what you need to know.

CPAP Battery Backup Checklist

  • Check your machine's wattage: Look on the power brick or manual. Most CPAPs draw 30-60W without humidifier.
  • Skip the humidifier during outages: It doubles power draw. Use it only if medically necessary.
  • Use a DC converter if possible: Some CPAP models have a DC input that bypasses the AC-to-DC conversion loss, extending battery life by 15-20%.
  • Keep your machine plugged into the power station: Use UPS mode (if available) so it automatically switches during an outage.
  • Buy 1.5x your nightly needs: If your CPAP uses 300 Wh per night, get at least 450 Wh capacity. This gives you buffer for longer outages or higher pressure settings.

What Not to Do

  • Don't use a car inverter + extension cord setup. It's unsafe and inefficient. The inverter drains your car battery and CO from idling in a garage is deadly.
  • Don't rely on USB power banks. Most CPAP machines need pure sine wave AC power. USB adapters won't work.
  • Don't wait until an outage to test your setup. Test it on a normal night first — make sure the power station's fan noise doesn't disturb your sleep, and verify the runtime matches your expectations.
